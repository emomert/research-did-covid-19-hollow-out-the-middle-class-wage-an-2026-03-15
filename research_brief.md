Established findings
- OECD and peer-reviewed evidence supports that COVID-19 interacted with pre-existing inequality and produced uneven impacts across population groups.
- Post-pandemic labor-market recovery did not eliminate distributional stress: inflation and affordability pressures remained central for household welfare.
- Consumption inequality dynamics can be non-linear: evidence from high-quality official data (U.S. comparator) shows an initial 2020 decline in measured consumption inequality followed by partial rebound, driven by category-specific spending shocks.

Provisional claims (supported, but not yet fully nailed down with directly accessible OECD microdata)
- The strongest version of the hypothesis (middle-class 'hollowing out' into both lower and upper segments across OECD) is plausible but not fully proven by the accessible full texts gathered here.
- Wage inequality within the middle class likely widened in some countries via sectoral scarring, bargaining asymmetries, and inflation pass-through, but this needs direct decile/ventile evidence specifically for middle-income households across OECD countries.
- Consumption inequality within the OECD middle class likely rose after emergency support faded and inflation hit essentials, but existing directly accessed OECD documents in this run do not provide a clean cross-country middle-class decomposition.

Recommended downscoping for publication-quality certainty
- Strongly defensible scope: 'COVID-19 intensified distributional pressure on OECD middle-income households, with heterogeneous wage and consumption effects across countries.'
- Avoid overclaiming universal 'hollowing out' unless supplemented with directly accessible OECD microdata tables (income-transition matrices, middle-income share shifts, decile consumption trajectories).
- If the exact title is retained, clearly label the hollowing-out claim as a testable proposition and present current evidence as mixed/partial rather than definitive.
